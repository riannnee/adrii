DATA

Data is defined as unstructured information such as text, observations, images, symbols, and descriptions on the other hand, Information refers to processed, organized, and structured data. It gives context to the facts and facilitates decision-making. Data contains numbers, statements, and characters in a raw form.

Two types of Data:

•Quantitative: Quantitative data refers to numerical information like weight, height, etc

•Qualitative: Qualitative data refers to non-numeric information like opinions, perceptions, etc.


INFORMATION

Information is defined as structured, organized, and processed data, presented within a context that makes it relevant and useful to the person who needs it. Data suggests that raw facts and figures regarding individuals, places, or the other issue, that is expressed within the type of numbers, letters or symbols. In short, once knowledge ends up being purposeful when conversing, it’s referred to as info. It’s one thing that informs, in essence, it provides a solution to a specific question. It may be obtained from numerous sources like newspapers, the internet, television, people, books, etc.


INFORMATION SYSTEM

An information system (IS) is an interconnected set of components used to collect, store, process and transmit data and digital information. At its core, it is a collection of hardware, software, data, people and processes that work together to transform raw data into useful information. An IS supports a variety of business objectives, such as improved customer service or increased operational efficiency. In our tech-dependent world, information systems often require a combination of both people and computers to function optimally. They not only provide, organize, and analyze data, but also present valuable information in a way that helps businesses make impactful decisions.

► MANAGEMENT INFORMATION SYSTEMS

A management information system (MIS) gives comprehensive, necessary insight into specificcircumstances. These daily, monthly, quarterly,or annual reports often consist of different kinds of data that help middle or senior managers problem-solve and make impactful business decisions.

► DECISION SUPPORT SYSTEMS

Similar to an MIS, a decision support system(DSS) helps managers make data-driven decisions. As an interactive information system, it collects and preserves large amounts of information that may be needed to support the reasoning behind certain business choices. Managers can use DSS reports to compare and contrast information, as they sometimes provide charts or tables for easy analysis


► TRANSACTION PROCESSING SYSTEMS

A transaction processing system (TPS) stores, processes, and distributes transactional data so that designated employees can easily access information when needed. Some examples of transactional data include bank deposits, payroll, sales, and other expenses that help maintain daily operations

► EXECUTIVE SUPPORT SYSTEMS

An Executive support system (ESS) is also commonly used by higher-level executives when they need to lead company activities. It collects data from a DSS and MIS, then presents both internal and external information that decisionmakers can use for their strategy.


ANALYSIS / REACTION

The rapid advancement of technology has prompted organizations to increasingly rely on it for more efficient and effective ways to achieve their goals. Across every industry— education, business, music, politics, and daily life—technology empowers people and organizations to collect data, process information, and make well-informed decisions. Information systems play a crucial role in analyzing and sharing information among diverse groups, ensuring individuals receive and utilize the information necessary for success.
Although often used interchangeably, data and information have distinct meanings. Data comprise raw, unprocessed facts—such as characters, images, symbols, or numbers—that can be either quantitative or qualitative. Information, by contrast, is data that has been processed and organized to become meaningful and useful to humans. For instance, a list of sales transactions represents data; when analyzed and processed, this data transforms into valuable information, enabling companies to assess business progress and improve strategies

In today’s digital world, organizations also depend on information systems to translate data into information. An information system (IS) refers to an interconnected set of components (hardware, software, people, data, and processes) that is used to collect, store, and transmit information. The components of information systems include hardware (people can physically see and touch), software (provides a list of commands and functions), database (a collection of relevant data), network (an interconnected system that exchanges electronic data), and human resources (people with technical skills). There are different types of support systems that can cover the needs of various people and organizations. A Transaction Processing System (TPS) is used by the operational-level system that stores, processes, and distributes transactional data for easier access and keeps track of incoming and outgoing transactions. A Management Information System (MIS) ensures the smooth operation of the organization by giving comprehensive, necessary insights, and is commonly used by middle managers. A Decision Support System (DSS) is often used by senior-level management to analyze existing structured information, compare information, and help support why certain decisions are necessary.

An Executive Information System (EIS) helps executives and senior managers analyze longterm trends, collect data from DSS and MSS, and aid businesses in achieving stronger decisionmaking. Together, these systems work to support organizations and move towards their goals. Through different information systems, people can connect with each other and cooperate to succeed. I understood that an IS does not simply handle technical information, it improves productivity, fosters better communication, supports better decision-making skills, thus leading to organizational growth. Understanding these terms and concepts helped me realize how technology bridges the gap between information and effective decision-making. Information systems turn meaningless data into valuable information that, in turn, allows individuals to construct decisions based on clear evidence and statistics rather than speculation.

Moreover, managing information requires not only technical skills but also the ability to ask the right questions and apply them responsibly and efficiently. Learning how these work is critical to adapt and maximize the potential of technology, and empowers everyone to be a critical user of information and technology. In my own experience, I have applied these skills in projects where data analysis was needed, including our research project regarding the financial literacy of students.

Information systems have significantly contributed to the success of organizations by allowing users to interact with technology in a manner that simplifies work and helps leaders enhance their decision-making and problem-solving skills. Without these data, information, and systems working together, businesses and institutions would struggle to adapt, compete, and grow. As a student and a future professional, I recognize the importance of equipping myself with this knowledge to become a capable and efficient user of information and technology in this continuously evolving world.
